the challenge

What problem did we solve?

Early detection of diabetes is critical to mitigating severe health complications, but clinical benchmark data often contains biologically implausible gaps — missing or zero entries in crucial insulin and blood pressure metrics — which cause standard classification algorithms to output misleading medical risk predictions.

what we built

How did we solve it?

We implemented a data-clearing pipeline using targeted mean and median imputation alongside Interquartile Range outlier capping to stabilize data variance. The preprocessed metrics were then evaluated across multiple non-parametric ensemble models, with Gradient Boosting achieving the highest overall accuracy at 76% and optimal balance of sensitivity for early-stage risk isolation.

what i learned

What did this teach me?

I went into this project thinking data cleaning was the boring part you get through quickly before the interesting modeling begins. I was completely wrong. The moment I started actually looking at the dataset — zeroes in blood pressure fields, insulin readings that made no biological sense — I realized that garbage data doesn't just lower your accuracy, it makes your entire model untrustworthy for real-world use. That clicked something for me: in health applications especially, how you prepare the data is the most responsible thing you can do. I also got much more comfortable with evaluating models beyond just accuracy — understanding sensitivity versus specificity, and why a model that misses actual diabetic patients is far more dangerous than one that over-flags healthy ones.
