the challenge

What problem did we solve?

Standard productivity apps are often too rigid, text-heavy, or complicated for neurodiverse individuals. Users with executive dysfunction experience stress, guilt, and burnout from sudden notifications and unstructured task lists — the very tools meant to help them end up making things worse.

what we built

How did we solve it?

We designed a cross-platform interface that begins with a built-in clinical screening test to adaptively tune distraction-blocking features. The workspace features a visual circular focus timer, a favorites-enabled ambient sound deck, cross-device blocking synchronization, a task list prioritized by urgency, and a "Locked Mode" that prevents breaking focus until the current cycle finishes.

what i learned

What did this teach me?

FocusPilot was the first time I had to design for someone whose cognitive experience is fundamentally different from mine — and that distinction forced a level of empathy I hadn't applied to design before. We did actual research into ADHD and executive dysfunction before touching Figma, and that changed everything. Features I would have considered "obvious" UX, like notification pop-ups and mandatory onboarding flows, turned out to be exactly what causes breakdowns for ADHD users. I learned that good design isn't about what looks clean or feels intuitive to you — it's about who you're actually designing for. Prototyping in Figma also pushed me to think about interaction states, micro-animations, and how a user physically moves through an app, not just what it looks like as a static screen.
