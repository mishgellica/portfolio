the challenge

What problem did we solve?

Relying on manual logbooks, disconnected spreadsheets, and social media messaging for customer appointments causes overlapping bookings, mismanaged technician assignments, blind spots in real-time inventory tracking, and vulnerable sales accounting records. Local businesses run like this lose money quietly in ways they can't even measure.

what we built

How did we solve it?

We engineered a multi-subsystem full-stack platform that centralizes salon operations. It features a custom appointment builder that coordinates technician availability with room capacities, tracks dynamic material consumption per service via complex many-to-many database pivot tables, and digitizes transaction records into live financial charts for the branch manager.

what i learned

What did this teach me?

This was the first project where I had to think seriously about database design beyond just "store the data." The many-to-many pivot tables for tracking which materials get consumed per service, per technician, per appointment — that structure took several iterations to get right, and every time we changed it we had to rewrite half the logic downstream. It taught me to slow down and model the data correctly before writing application code, because the database schema is essentially your commitment to how the entire system thinks. I also learned what it means to build for a real business owner — not a professor grading a rubric, but someone who needs to close their books at the end of the day. That shift in who you're building for changes everything about your priorities.
